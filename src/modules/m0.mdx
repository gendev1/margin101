### What an option literally is

An **option** is a contract about a stock (the *underlying*).
It gives its **buyer** a right, and puts its **seller** under
an obligation:

| | Buyer (long) gets the right to… | Seller (short) is obligated to… |
|---|---|---|
| **Call** | buy 100 shares at a fixed price K | sell 100 shares at K if asked |
| **Put** | sell 100 shares at a fixed price K | buy 100 shares at K if asked |

The buyer pays the seller a price for this right, called the **premium**.
The right expires on a fixed date. One standard contract covers **100 shares** —
that 100 is the *multiplier*, and it is why every formula in the rulebook ends
with `* qty * mult`: option prices are quoted per share, but obligations are
per contract.

<Callout kind="tip">

A call is “I might want to buy from you at K.” A put is “I might want to sell to you
at K.” The buyer exercises only when it benefits them — so the seller participates
only in the losing side of the bet. That asymmetry is the entire reason margin exists.

</Callout>

### Moneyness: ITM, OTM, intrinsic value

**Intrinsic value** is what exercising would be worth *right now*:

<Formula>{`intrinsic_call(K, U) = max(0, U − K)   // right to buy at K, worth U−K when U above K
intrinsic_put(K, U)  = max(0, K − U)   // right to sell at K, worth K−U when U below K`}</Formula>

- **ITM (in the money)** — intrinsic \> 0. A 90-strike call with the stock at 95 is $5 ITM.
- **OTM (out of the money)** — intrinsic = 0. Same call with the stock at 85 is $5 OTM.
- **ATM (at the money)** — U ≈ K.

Why does an OTM option cost anything at all? Because U can still move before expiry.
Premium = intrinsic value + time value. Margin formulas mostly care about intrinsic —
the `max(0, …)` patterns you see everywhere in the YAML are intrinsic-value
and OTM-distance calculations.

### The two account types

| | Cash account | Margin account |
|---|---|---|
| **Idea** | Pay for everything in full, up front. The broker takes no credit risk. | The broker extends credit against collateral. Positions can be partially financed. |
| **Consequence** | Risky structures are simply *not permitted* (you'll see `permitted: false` in the cash blocks). | Everything is permitted if you post enough collateral — the rulebook computes how much. |

Every rule in `cboe_baseline.yaml` has separate `cash:` and
`margin:` formula blocks for exactly this reason.

### Initial vs maintenance — the two questions margin answers

- **Initial requirement** — how much equity must you have to *open* the
  position? Set by Regulation T (the Fed) — the famous “50% for stock.” Uses `P0`,
  the premiums at open.
- **Maintenance requirement** — how much equity must you have to *keep* it,
  re-checked continuously as prices move? Set by FINRA/exchange rules — “25% for long stock.”
  Uses `P`, current premiums. Fall below it and the broker issues a
  **margin call**: deposit more or be liquidated.

### The three numbers limen returns

Every evaluation produces a `Result` with three dollar figures. Worked
example — the manual's p.42 vertical call spread:

<Panel title="Result anatomy — vertical call spread, Cboe manual p.42">
  <Ledger>
    <LedgerRow label="Requirement (gross)" value="$880.00" />
    <LedgerRow label="AppliedProceeds (premium received)" value="− $840.00" tone="pos" />
    <LedgerRow label="CashCall (what you must deposit)" value="$40.00" tone="neg" total />
  </Ledger>
</Panel>

- **Requirement** — the gross collateral the rule demands. This matches the
  number printed in the Cboe manual (“Margin Requirement: $880”).
- **AppliedProceeds** — money you *received* for selling options, credited
  against the requirement. When you sell (write) an option, the premium lands in your account
  immediately — the rules let it offset what you owe.
- **CashCall = Requirement − AppliedProceeds** — the net cash that must actually
  come out of your pocket.

<Callout>

The manual sometimes displays the gross number, sometimes the net. limen always returns
all three so the integrating service can present whichever convention it needs. When you
compare against any external number, first establish *which of the three* it is.

</Callout>

### Why margin exists at all (the broker's problem)

When a customer shorts an option, the broker is on the hook if the customer can't pay.
The margin requirement answers one question from the broker's seat:
*“if this position moves against the customer overnight, how much of their own money
must already be in the account so I don't eat the loss?”* Every formula you will see
is some estimate of “a bad move” — 20% of the underlying, the maximum possible loss of a
spread, 10% of a strike — chosen by regulators to be conservative but tradable.

### The vocabulary limen deliberately doesn't own — yet

You will hear these words around every margin conversation. They are *real*, they
are on the platform map, and limen intentionally computes none of them today. Knowing
which side of the boundary each lives on is itself rulebook literacy:

| Term | What it is | Where it lives |
|---|---|---|
| **Equity** | account value minus what's borrowed — the customer's own money at stake | account aggregation (built) |
| **Excess equity** | equity above the maintenance requirement — the cushion | derivable from limen's outputs |
| **SMA** | the Reg-T “Special Memorandum Account” — a day-over-day ledger of credits that determines what you may buy next. The manual's “SMA debit $40” on p.42 is this. | *not built* — a stateful ledger on top of CashCall (plane 5) |
| **Buying power** | how much the customer can purchase right now, derived from SMA/excess equity (≈2× for Reg-T stock) | *not built* — same ledger |
| **Margin call types** | Fed call (initial shortfall), maintenance call, day-trade call — different deadlines and remedies | *not built* — host workflow (plane 5) |
| **Settlement / assignment** | exercise picks a short at random via the OCC; T+1 stock settlement timing | upstream of limen — positions arrive already settled |

<Callout kind="warn">

The trap this table prevents: someone asks “can limen tell me the buying power?” and the
tempting answer is “sure, it's basically excess equity times two.” It is not — buying
power depends on the SMA ledger, which has *memory* (yesterday's balance carries
forward and never shrinks from market moves alone). A requirements engine can't answer
a ledger question, and pretending it can is the silent-wrong-number failure mode applied
to account state. limen computes the *requirement events*; the ledger that
interprets them is a separate, stateful system.

</Callout>

### Self-check

<Reveal q="You are short 3 XYZ 50-strike puts, premium $1.20, stock at $54. Translate every symbol.">

side=short, kind=option, option_type=put, K=50, P=1.20, qty=3, mult=100, U=54.
The put is OTM by $4 (intrinsic_put = max(0, 50−54) = 0). You received
3 × 100 × $1.20 = $360 of premium, and you are obligated to buy 300 shares at $50
if assigned.

</Reveal>

<Reveal q="A call with K=120 when U=128.50 — ITM or OTM, and by how much?">

ITM by $8.50: intrinsic_call = max(0, 128.50 − 120) = 8.50. Per contract that's
$850 of intrinsic value.

</Reveal>

<Reveal q="Requirement $2,300, AppliedProceeds $550. What's the CashCall, and what does it mean physically?">

$1,750. The rule demands $2,300 of collateral; $550 of option premium you collected
counts toward it; you must come up with the remaining $1,750 in cash or equity.

</Reveal>

<Reveal q="Why can't limen compute buying power today, in one sentence?">

Buying power comes from the SMA ledger, which carries state forward day over day —
limen is a pure function over a snapshot and deliberately has no memory; the ledger is
a separate plane-5 system that consumes limen's CashCall events.

</Reveal>
