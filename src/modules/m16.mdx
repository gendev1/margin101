The engine prices a *named structure*: hand it legs that form a covered call, get a number. But customers don't submit named structures — they submit a bag of legs. Who decides that these 100 shares pair with *that* short call instead of standing alone? The **decomposition optimizer**. It exists because the pairing decision is worth real money, and the customer is entitled to the best legal one. Taught from zero, with every number computable from [single-leg pricing](#m2).

### The problem, concretely

One account, four legs, U = $100, plain equity:

<Formula>{`leg 1:  long 100 shares
leg 2:  short 1 call  K=105, P=$2.00
leg 3:  short 1 put   K=95,  P=$1.50`}</Formula>

Price each piece standalone first (single-leg math — verify these yourself): stock alone = 0.50 × 100 × 100 = **$5,000**; naked 105-call = (2 + max(20−5, 10)) × 100 = **$1,700**; naked 95-put = (1.5 + max(20−5, 9.5)) × 100 = **$1,650**. Now — same legs, three legal groupings, three different totals:

  <thead><tr><th>Partition</th><th>Groups</th><th>Arithmetic</th><th>Total</th></tr></thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>A</strong></td>
      <td>covered_call(shares + call) · naked put</td>
      <td>5,000 (call rides free) + 1,650</td>
      <td><strong>$6,650</strong> ✓</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>B</strong></td>
      <td>strangle(put + call) · long stock</td>
      <td>max(1,650, 1,700) + 150 + 5,000</td>
      <td>$6,850</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td><strong>C</strong></td>
      <td>everything standalone</td>
      <td>5,000 + 1,700 + 1,650</td>
      <td>$8,350</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

Partition A wins: pairing the shares with the call makes the call's requirement vanish (assignment just takes shares you own — [stock hedges](#m4)). The customer with partition C's broker posts **$1,700 more for the identical risk**. That gap is the optimizer's entire reason to exist — and why it's not optional polish: first-match alone would price whatever grouping the input happened to arrive in.

### Why this is a search problem (and why that's hard)

The number of ways to partition n legs grows like the Bell numbers: 4 legs → 15 ways, 8 legs → 4,140, 12 legs → 4,213,597. Trying them all stops being feasible fast. The classic answer is **branch and bound (B&amp;B)** — from zero:

<Formula>{`grow a tree: at each node, take the next leg and try every way to
place it — into an existing group or starting a new one.

                        {}
            ┌────────────┴────────────┐
        {sh}                       …
     ┌────┴─────────┐
 {sh,call}       {sh} {call}            ← "branch": every placement
     │                │
 priced: $5,000   priced: $5,000+1,700  ← partial cost so far
     │                ✗ PRUNED: already ≥ best-known $6,650
     ▼
 keep exploring only branches that could still win

"bound" = if a partial grouping already costs more than the best
complete answer found so far, every completion of it loses too —
cut the whole subtree without pricing it.`}</Formula>

- **Branch** = enumerate placements. **Bound** = requirements only grow as you add groups, so a partial total is a floor for every completion — subtrees that can't beat the incumbent die unexplored.
- **Legality is delegated.** Every candidate group is priced by the same rule engine with the same guards — the optimizer can't invent a grouping the rulebook wouldn't accept (a “covered call” with mismatched underlying simply fails to bind). New discipline: search. Zero new margin math.
- **Residual sinks.** Legs no template absorbs fall to standalone rules (naked call, plain stock) — by explicit rule ID, *after* the search, never as shortcuts inside it. Your locked D6/D7 ([your decisions: the number](#m8)): if the cheap standalone rules were searchable targets, B&amp;B could “win” by leaving a call naked that shares could have covered.
- **The node budget.** Worst-case trees are still huge, so the search caps explored nodes. Under budget pressure it returns the best *found* — legal and safe, possibly not cheapest. Customer-fair degradation, not unsoundness.

### Self-check

<Reveal q="Add a 4th leg to the worked account: long 1 put K=90 @ $1. Which partition should win now, roughly?">
  <p>The long 90-put can cap the short 95-put into a vertical: min(naked 1,650, MPL 500) +
    100 paid = $600 — versus $1,650 naked + $100 standalone long. New best: covered
    call (5,000) + put vertical (600) = <strong>$5,600</strong>, beating A's 6,650 + 100.
    One added hedge leg, $1,150 cheaper — and the optimizer must <em>find</em> that pairing
    among 15+ partitions.</p>
</Reveal>
<Reveal q="Why does the bound argument require that adding a group never lowers the total?">
  <p>Pruning says: partial cost ≥ best-known ⟹ abandon. That's only valid if completing a
    partition can't <em>reduce</em> cost — true because every group's requirement is ≥ 0.
    If a group could price negative, a losing partial could still win and pruning would be
    unsound. (This is also why requirement-reducing modes don't exist anywhere: they'd
    break the optimizer's math, not just the audit story.)</p>
</Reveal>
<Reveal q="The optimizer returns a partition priced $7,200; exhaustive enumeration says $6,650 existed. Bug class?">
  <p>A search bug, not a pricing bug — every group was priced correctly; the search failed
    to reach the better partition (bad pruning bound, budget exhaustion order, or a missing
    branch). This is exactly the class the decompose corpus exists to catch, and why its
    oracle must be exhaustive rather than spot-check.</p>
</Reveal>
