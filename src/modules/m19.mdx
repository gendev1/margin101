Vendor margin systems compute house calls **once a day, after the close** — and even a perfectly reliable EOD-only system has a structural blind spot: a customer can be fine at 9:30, deeply under water at 11:00, and the firm finds out at 6pm. **Intraday streaming re-evaluation** closes that gap: recompute margin continuously as prices move, and warn *before* the call exists. Gray on the map, host-buildable on today's SDK — here's the actual shape of the system.

### The naive version, and why it almost works

<Formula>{`every N seconds:
  for each account:
    re-evaluate everything (engine + optimizer + aggregation + overlay)
    if equity < threshold × requirement → alert

cost check: 0.12 ms/position × 100,000 positions ≈ 12 s of ONE core
            → a 16-core box re-margins the whole firm every ~1s`}</Formula>

Because limen is a pure function with no I/O, the brute-force loop is already almost viable — that's unusual, and it's a direct dividend of the purity decisions ([your system decisions](#m9)). Most margin systems can't do this because their calculation is entangled with their database. The refinements are about not wasting the 99% of work that recomputes unchanged answers:

- **Dirty-marking by underlying.** A tick in XYZ only moves accounts holding XYZ. Index accounts by underlying; a tick re-evaluates the affected slice, not the firm. The fan-out is exactly the `U` input changing and nothing else.
- **Debounce by materiality.** A $0.01 tick doesn't need a re-margin; a 2% move does. Threshold the trigger, not just the alert.
- **Stateless workers scale sideways.** Every evaluation is independent — shard accounts across workers with zero coordination. (Compare a vendor's nightly batch: a singleton job that fails as a unit. The architectural opposite.)

### The product: early warning, not just faster calls

<Formula>{`equity / requirement:   1.50      1.25      1.10       1.00      <1.00
                         │         │         │          │          │
                       healthy   watch    WARN the   margin     forced
                                 list     customer   call       liquidation
                                          "you're 10% from
                                           a call at U=$87"`}</Formula>

The valuable output isn't “the call fired three hours earlier.” It's the **warning band**: telling a customer at 1.10× that a further 4% drop in their concentrated position triggers a call — while they can still act voluntarily. Better for the customer (they choose what to trim), better for the firm (voluntary de-risking beats forced liquidation into a falling market). The math for “at what U does this account hit 1.00×?” is a root-find over the same evaluation — one more place the pure engine gets reused as a subroutine.

### What's genuinely hard (it's not the compute)

- **Intraday inputs are worse.** EOD has settled closing prices; intraday has last-trade prints that can be stale, crossed, or halted. A re-margin off a bad print can fire a false alarm at scale. Input quality gates (staleness, sanity bands) matter more than evaluation speed.
- **Alert hygiene is a product problem.** An account oscillating around 1.10× must not page anyone forty times. Hysteresis (alert at 1.10 falling, clear at 1.15 rising) is the difference between a tool and a nuisance.
- **It must agree with EOD.** Same rulebook hash, same overrides, same refdata as the official run — an intraday number that disagrees with the EOD number for the same prices destroys trust in both. (Same consistency argument as pre-trade, [pre-trade](#m18) — these two systems share their hardest requirement.)

### Self-check

<Reveal q="Why does limen's purity make the brute-force loop feasible at all?">
  <p>No I/O or hidden state means evaluation cost is pure CPU (~0.12 ms) and
    embarrassingly parallel — 100k positions is seconds on one box. A calculation that
    reads its own database per evaluation would be 100× slower and contend with itself;
    the architecture, not the formula speed, is what makes streaming viable.</p>
</Reveal>

<Reveal q="A symbol halts at $40 after trading at $80. What should the intraday system do, and what must it NOT do?">
  <p>Freeze the symbol's accounts with a “stale/halted input” status and route to humans.
    It must NOT re-margin everyone at $40 and fire mass liquidation warnings off a print
    that may reopen at $75 — input-quality gating before evaluation. A wrong number
    delivered fast is still a wrong number; the fail-loud philosophy applies to market
    data exactly as it applies to a missing rates class.</p>
</Reveal>

<Reveal q="Why must intraday and EOD share the same rulebook hash?">
  <p>If they can disagree for identical inputs, every discrepancy becomes “which system is
    wrong?” — and the answer is undiagnosable because they're running different rules. Pin
    both to the same content hash and any intraday-vs-EOD difference is, by construction,
    an input difference (prices, overrides), which is exactly the difference the system
    exists to surface.</p>
</Reveal>
